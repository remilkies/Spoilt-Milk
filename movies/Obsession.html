Spoilt Review

First of all, Curry Barker absolutely cooked with Obsession, easily one of the most unsettling and brilliant films to hit screens this year. But underneath the horror, this film says everything about how comfortable men are with blatantly coercing women and capitalising on a situation while genuine consent is entirely impossible. (for the people thinking "not all men", congradulations for having an open mind, have a nice day o7) The genius of Obsession is that the supernatural curse is just a mirror for real-world gender dynamics. It perfectly captures how entitlement masquerades as romance. The "main character", Bear, doesn't want a human being; he wants compliance. Watching that play out— where a lack of autonomy is treated as a win, followed by a total refusal to take accountability when things shatter— makes it one of the most brilliant, uncomfortable relationship horrors in years. But then again, it’s just a movie right :P

ok no. "it's just a movie" is a cope we tell ourselves so we can actually sleep at night once the horror binge is over. The best horror doesn't invent new monsters out of nowhere; it just takes a very real, very mundane societal rot and puts a supernatural lens over it so we're forced to stare it right in the face. Bear’s entire "timid, nice guy" routine is just a mask for total entitlement. The horror of the movie isn't just supernatural. It’s the fact that Bear is perfectly willing to treat a complete lack of genuine agency as "consent" as long as it gets him what he wants, only to immediately feign ignorance, refuse to take accountability and believe himself to be innocent and a victim ("I had no idea it would go this far!") when the house starts burning down. Men like Bear hide behind the excuse of "passive compliance" or "cluelessness" but we HAVE to acknowledge that the coercion wasn't some subtle, accidental misunderstanding. It was an active, aggressive choice disguised as a shrug. The subtext here is vicious. The Anatomy of the "Innocent Perpetrator" isn't just a terrifying layer of psychological horror in film, but immitates real life: The Intentional Blindspot: They aren't just lying to the world when they say "I didn't think it would turn out like this", they're actively gaslighting themselves. They construct a reality where their desires are so paramount that the destruction left in their wake is treated like an unavoidable natural disaster rather than a consequence of their actions. Weaponized Victimhood: The shift from perpetrator to victim is the ultimate defensive maneuver. By convincing themselves they're the ones suffering, they completely absolve themselves of the need to feel guilt, offer a genuine apology, or face accountability. Horror is at its absolute best when it acts as a trojan horse for social critique. Obsession gives people the thrill of a supernatural curse, but leaves them sitting with the deeply uncomfortable reality of everyday entitlement and the fragility of male accountability.
