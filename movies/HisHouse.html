The Horror of Erasure: His House

Good horror doesn’t just jump-scare; it forces us to confront the rot in our society by casting it as a monster. In His House, the real "haunting" isn’t just the entity within the walls—it is the systematic dismantling of a person’s identity in exchange for the "privilege" of safety.

The False Promise of "Assimilation"

The film highlights a deeply hypocritical demand: society expects asylum seekers to shed their pasts and "be reborn" as palatable, productive members of a new culture. The Knife and Fork Paradox: Someting as simple as eating with a knife and fork is a point we revisit througout the film. What looks simple "why doesn't she just use a knife and fork" is a society demanding for culture to be erased to even stand a single chance at safety. It serves as a microcosm of the entire assimilation struggle. Using a knife and fork isn't just about table manners; it is a forced performance of "civilization." It’s a demand that they discard their culture to prove they are human enough to deserve basic housing. The "Tolerable" Victim: The government isn't asking for refugees to be happy; they are asking them to be tolerable. They are expected to be grateful for the "unseasoned scraps"—dilapidated housing in neglected neighborhoods—while effectively suppressing the PTSD and the grief of the lives they were forced to leave behind. 2. The Weight of Memory vs. The Demand to Forget The difference between those who arrive as refugees and those who have historically taken land. The Double Standard: Settler-colonial societies often carry their beliefs and cultures with them, viewing them as intrinsic rights. Conversely, refugees are expected to arrive as blank slates. They are forced to live with the profound trauma of losing their homes and their kin, yet they are denied the psychiatric assistance and empathy necessary to process that loss. The Price of Survival: The film asks a harrowing question: What is a being without a soul? When the host society demands you erase your heritage, your language, and your customs to "fit in," you aren't being saved—you are being hollowed out. 3. The House as a Trap Ultimately, the house is a physical manifestation of this tragedy. It is supposed to be a place of refuge, but it becomes a site of further confinement. The protagonists are trapped there because the system has left them with no other option. They are expected to navigate the horror of the entity in the walls while simultaneously navigating the cold, indifferent horror of a bureaucracy that views them as "lucky" to be alive, regardless of the cost. Reflection The tragedy of the film—that the characters are portrayed as "difficult" for refusing to erase their souls—is the film's most biting social commentary. It forces the audience to realize that the pressure to assimilate isn't a gesture of welcome; it is a condition of existence.
